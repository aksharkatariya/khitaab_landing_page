A farm ledger you talk to.

Khitaab keeps track of your farm's money. You send it a voice note or a message on Telegram, in your own language, and it records the entry, answers questions, and builds reports you can take to a bank. No app to install, no forms, no accounting.

Everything works through things you already know how to send

There is no interface to learn. Khitaab is a chat, and each kind of message you send it unlocks a different capability. Here is the full list today.

A voice note input 1 of 4

Press the mic and say what happened: "kal 800 rupaye ki khaad kharidi" or "sold wheat for 4,500." Khitaab understands speech in nine Indian languages, using Gemini's speech models, and logs the entry with the date, amount, and category. This is the input we care about most. Most of the farmers we work with prefer speaking to typing, and none of them should need English to keep books.

A typed message input 2 of 4

Type the same things instead, in your language's script or in Roman letters. "Spent 1200 on diesel" and "1200 ka diesel" both work. You can also ask questions: what did I spend on seed this season, how much profit did the last harvest make, should I take this loan given my numbers.

A photo of your field input 3 of 4 · uses satellite data

Send one photo taken at your field with location switched on. Khitaab reads the coordinates, queries Google Earth Engine, and replies with the last 30 days of land surface temperature and rainfall for the kilometre around that point. One photo, and your ledger knows where your farm is.

Photos of your field's corners input 4 of 4 · in testing

Walk your field's boundary and take a photo at each corner. Khitaab joins those points into a polygon, which means it knows your field's exact shape and area. From there the analysis stops being about a point on a map and starts being about your land specifically: soil moisture, rainfall, temperature, and eventually crop suitability, measured inside your boundary and nowhere else.

Why a ledger, of all things

Most smallholder farmers in India keep no written accounts, and that gap follows them into every bank branch. Without records there is no credit history. Without credit history, formal loans are slow or refused, and the moneylender fills the space.

Khitaab's job is to make the record exist as a side effect of talking. Every voice note becomes a clean entry. Every season becomes a statement. When it is time to apply for a loan or a subsidy, Khitaab produces a bank-ready PDF of your farm's finances, built from your own words.

We are building this in the open with a small group of pilot farmers. If the numbers on this page look modest, that is because they are real.
